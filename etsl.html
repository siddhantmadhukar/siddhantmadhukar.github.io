Energy and Transport Sciences Lab - Purdue

I am a Graduate Researcher at the Energy and Transport Sciences (ETS) Lab, Purdue University. My current work focuses on thermal management for AI microchips, exploring the fundamental heat transfer mechanisms at the micro-level and developing strategies to optimize their performance. As I am in the early stages of this research, much of my work involves brainstorming, conceptual development, and exploratory modeling to establish new directions. In parallel, I am also working on computational models for thermal management in battery energy storage systems (BESS), aiming to improve efficiency, reliability, and long-term sustainability.

Note: This research is ongoing. Results are preliminary, and detailed findings will be shared once validated.

Project Overview

Challenge

The rapid scaling of artificial intelligence (AI) hardware has led to chips operating at unprecedented power densities, producing intense heat in compact geometries. Conventional cooling strategies struggle at the micro- and nano-scale, where thermal resistance, non-uniform hotspots, and material limits present significant bottlenecks. Similarly, battery energy storage systems (BESS) face growing demands for efficiency and safety under high charging/discharging cycles. Poor thermal management can accelerate degradation, reduce reliability, and even trigger catastrophic failures. Balancing performance, energy efficiency, and long-term reliability across these domains requires innovative thermal modeling approaches that bridge fundamental physics with scalable engineering solutions.

Solution

At ETS Lab, my work is directed toward developing computational thermal models that integrate detailed physics-based simulations with data-driven methods. For AI microchips, this involves investigating micro-level heat transport mechanisms, designing enhanced cooling strategies, and benchmarking novel approaches such as phase-change materials and microfluidic channels. For BESS, I am building multi-scale thermal models to simulate cell-to-pack level heat transfer, optimize airflow/liquid cooling layouts, and explore predictive algorithms for real-time thermal management. This dual-track approach—microchip cooling and battery system modeling—lays the foundation for safer, more reliable, and energy-efficient technologies in both computing and energy storage.
